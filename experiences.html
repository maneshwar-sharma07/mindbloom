Experiences

1.Anxiety & Depression: Feeling disembodied and frantic

One person shared that from a young age, they would feel like they were floating outside of their body—unable to fully control themselves or understand what was happening. These episodes would come without warning and be filled with fear and confusion. For years, they thought something was deeply wrong with them. As they got older, they were eventually diagnosed with anxiety and depersonalization disorder. Through therapy, learning coping techniques, and expressing their emotions creatively (such as writing or music), they began to understand that these sensations were symptoms, not personal failings. Healing, for them, felt like finally being able to “come home” to their body and emotions. There were still bad days, but now they had tools to manage them.

2. Depression Hidden Behind a Successful Exterior

A man who appeared successful in his job, family life, and finances described waking up every day with a heavy sense of dread. On the outside, he was a model citizen—but on the inside, he felt like he was crumbling. He said it felt like drowning in plain sight. He reached a breaking point when suicidal thoughts became frequent. He finally opened up to a therapist. With time, he learned that his perfectionism and constant pressure to “be strong” were contributing to his mental decline. Healing came through learning to show vulnerability, practicing self-compassion, and prioritizing mental wellness over achievements. He now says he feels more emotionally present with his children and finally understands what it means to be truly alive.

3. Severe Depression Leading to Complete Withdrawal

One young person described a time in their life when they would sleep 14 to 16 hours a day, isolate themselves in their room, and avoid all social contact. They stopped showering, eating regularly, and lost interest in everything. They turned to alcohol and smoking as a way to numb the pain. They described their mind as a dark tunnel with no exit. It was only when they visited a psychiatrist that things began to shift. They were diagnosed with clinical depression and started treatment with medication and therapy. Over time, their energy levels improved. They started making small changes—like walking outside once a day—and gradually built a routine. Healing didn’t happen overnight, but they eventually found a sense of stability and joy they hadn’t felt in years.

4.Finding Hope Through Group Therapy

Another person shared how they had always felt like a burden to others and kept their depression to themselves. After a traumatic life event, they spiraled into a deep depression. A friend encouraged them to attend a group therapy session. At first, they were hesitant and didn’t want to speak. But just listening to others who shared similar feelings was powerful. Over time, they began opening up and realized they weren’t alone. They felt validated, understood, and less ashamed. Healing, for them, was tied to community—finding people who truly “got it” and walking through recovery together.

5.Medication and Daily Habits as a Lifeline

One individual who suffered from panic attacks and depression found that medication helped create stability. They described the weeks after starting antidepressants as a fog lifting—thoughts became clearer, their sleep improved, and they could finally get through the day without overwhelming anxiety. They also began practicing daily self-care routines: Writing three things they were grateful for each day. Making a short weekly to-do list to regain a sense of control. Setting alarms for mindfulness breaks. Going to sleep and waking up at the same time daily. These habits didn’t cure them, but they gave them a structure and sense of purpose. They emphasized that healing is a combination of small wins, not one big moment.

6.PTSD and Flashbacks After Trauma

A teenager shared that after experiencing a traumatic event, they began having frequent flashbacks and anxiety attacks. Their heart would race, their face would flush, and they felt unsafe even in familiar environments. They avoided crowds and certain topics that reminded them of the event. Recovery for them included grounding exercises like holding onto cold objects, breathing deeply, and journaling. Therapy helped them process their trauma in a safe space. They said that while the memories never completely vanished, their power diminished. Healing felt like finally being able to breathe again without fear.

7.Therapist with Lived Experience

A mental health professional once shared that they themselves had considered suicide at one point in life. Despite their training, they found themselves spiraling after a series of life stressors. The pressure to appear “fine” because of their profession only made things worse. They checked into a residential treatment program and slowly rebuilt their emotional health. After recovering, they returned to therapy work with a new depth of empathy. They now believe their darkest moments gave them a unique ability to connect with others who are suffering. Healing for them wasn’t just survival—it became a purpose.

Common Emotional Experiences During the Struggle

  • Hopelessness: Feeling like the pain will never end.
  • Shame: Believing there’s something wrong or broken inside.
  • Isolation: Avoiding others, feeling alone even in a crowd.
  • Anxiety: Constant restlessness, racing heart, and intrusive thoughts.
  • Exhaustion: Mental and physical fatigue, even after sleep.
  • Disconnection: Feeling numb or detached from reality or the body.

What Healing Feels Like

  • Relief: Finally naming the problem and knowing it’s treatable.
  • Empowerment: Learning tools to manage emotions and triggers.
  • Connection: Feeling seen and understood by others.
  • Peace:Emotional regulation and deeper self-awareness.
  • Purpose: A renewed reason to live, often through helping others or creative expression.
  • Self-Compassion: Treating oneself with kindness instead of judgment
